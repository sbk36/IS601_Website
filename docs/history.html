The Invention of Internet

The Internet is a worldwide network that connects computers for the purpose of sharing information and communicating.It is also referred to as a twenty-first-century telephony.The internet, which dates back to the 1950s, has evolved swiftly and is now available to anyone with an internet service provider.In 1998, more emails were transmitted per day than first-class letters via the post office, accounting for 1/3 of the US economic growth.When computers were initially introduced, they were slow and the size of rooms.Over time, computers have become quicker and smaller, and they can now be used anywhere in the world.

 

J.C.R. Licklider was the individual who came up with the notion or "seed" of computers. During the civil war, the Advanced Research Project Agency, which managed the space program, which included computer science, began working on computers. Leonard Kleinrock began working on data transmission at MIT and is best known for queuing theory, an area of operations research with applications in a wide range of fields. He implemented two strategies:Demand access & Distributing control.

 

Robert Taylors' huge new idea in 1960 was time-sharing. Time-sharing is a technique of operation in which multiple users, each with their own software, interact with the main processing unit of a bigger digital computer. He sought to establish a network that would allow one terminal to communicate with all devices. He hired Lawrence Roberts to develop the first network after receiving the cash to work on it. In 1968, Larry assembled a team of engineers to create the ARPANET (Advanced Research Projects Agency Network), which is today known as the internet. This was built on Interface Message Processors, or IMPs, which were minicomputers or smaller computers, and a revolutionary technology called package switching. The intention was to connect identical IMPs, each of which would be connected to a different mainframe computer: Sigma 7 connected at UCLA, SDS 940 SRI connected at Stanford Research Institute, BM 360/75 connected at University of California, PDP 10 connected at University of Utah.

The ARPANET had 18 mainframe machines connected to it by April 1971. Emails and data were circulated throughout the network. The world-changing invention of Computer Networks was shown in Washington,DC in October 1972. Finally, a bill was approved by Congress that brought this out of the realm of exclusivity and into the public domain.
