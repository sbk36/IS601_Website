Dot Com Bubble

 

The computer's ingenuity, combined with the internet, has altered how the modern world will exist. This progression is far more significant than the economic impact of railroads and automobiles. It took 20 years for television and radio to become commonplace. It only took four years for the personal computer (pc) to reach 50 million users. As a result, there was a significant need for less expensive and quicker computers. A new producer was created as the number of online users grew faster than the demand could keep up with. Due to this new world of online buyers, companies like eBay and Amazon had stock valuations in the billions.

 

The stock market gained a new market as a result of this. Day traders at home were making more money than ever before. Most homes had a computer and internet connection by the 1990s. This new period saw a massive increase in startup businesses, with youngsters becoming millionaires overnight. In the 2000s, with the stock market at an all-time high, Federal Reserve Chairman Alan Greenspan tried to cool things down by hiking interest rates.

In 2001, the dot-com bubble burst, wiping out 3 1/2 trillion dollars in paper value in a single year. Thousands of dot com companies went out of business during the next few years, allowing more solid corporations like eBay and Amazon to continue to develop. Analysts say the outcome was worth it, even though the quick rise created new money and the crash destroyed many lives. In the case of e-commerce, a new infrastructure was constructed in five years rather than the usual 15 years.
